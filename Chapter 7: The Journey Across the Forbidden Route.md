# Chapter 7: The Journey Across the Forbidden Route

Morning arrived quietly over Dhanushkodi. The sea stretched wide and calm, glowing softly under the pale gold light of sunrise. Fishing boats rocked gently along the shore, and the village was just beginning to wake.

But for Harish and his friends, the day had already begun long before dawn. Today was different. Today they were leaving not for fishing, not for exploration along the coast, but for something far greater. An expedition.

Down by the harbor, Joseph’s boat waited. It was larger and stronger than the usual fishing vessels in the village. The hull had been reinforced for deeper waters, and several modern instruments were fixed near the navigation console. Joseph moved quickly across the deck, checking every detail: fuel levels, navigation equipment, diving tanks, and backup oxygen cylinders.

Harish walked up beside him. “Everything ready?”

Joseph nodded without looking up. “Just about.” He tightened the strap around one of the scuba tanks. “If we’re diving in open sea, we prepare for anything.”

Nearby, Sharan was crouched beside the navigation system. A small laptop connected to the boat’s sonar display flickered as he ran a series of checks. “Electronics are good,” he announced. “Sonar, depth radar, GPS—everything is working.”

Keerthi stood near the storage crates tying down several supply ropes. “Food supplies secured,” she said. “And diving gear packed.”

Abinaya approached slowly from the temple path. In her bag were two important things: the book about King Athiyan and the Maragatham gemstone. Behind her, the temple priest walked quietly. The friends turned respectfully when they saw him.

Joseph stepped forward. “Swami, we’re leaving now.”

The priest nodded gently. His calm eyes moved across the group. “You are chasing a story that has slept for centuries.” Then he looked at Abinaya. “Take this with you.”

He placed the Maragatham gemstone carefully in her hand. Abinaya blinked in surprise. “You want me to carry it?”

The priest nodded. “It may guide you when the time comes.”

Harish watched the exchange carefully. Something about it felt strange. The priest had spoken those words with quiet certainty, as if he knew something the others didn’t, as if the gemstone would play a larger role later. But the priest said nothing more. He simply stepped back.

Joseph climbed into the captain’s position. “Time to move.”

The boat engine roared softly to life. One by one, the ropes were released from the dock. The vessel slowly drifted away from the shore. The priest stood watching as the boat moved farther into the sea. Harish leaned over the railing and waved. “See you soon!”

The priest raised his hand in return, but his eyes held a quiet seriousness. As if he knew the sea ahead would test them.

The boat cut steadily through the morning waters. Dhanushkodi slowly disappeared behind them. For a while, the ocean felt peaceful. But the calm did not last. Joseph studied the navigation screen carefully. “We’re entering the rough section.”

Sharan looked up from the radar. “The reef zone?”

Joseph nodded. “This route is rarely used.”

Keerthi frowned. “Why?”

Joseph pointed toward the sonar display. The screen showed jagged formations beneath the surface. “Because the seabed here is full of reefs and rocks.”

Sharan added, “Sharp ones.”

Joseph continued steering carefully. “One wrong turn and a ship’s hull could tear open.”

Harish looked across the water. It looked calm, but beneath the surface, the ocean hid dangerous barriers. No wonder fishermen avoided this path. Sharan adjusted the navigation signals. “I’m tracking the reefs.”

Joseph nodded. “Guide me through them.”

The boat began moving slowly between invisible obstacles. Sometimes Joseph turned sharply to avoid hidden rock ridges. Other times Sharan warned him just seconds before reaching dangerous shallows. Keerthi kept moving across the deck, tightening ropes and securing equipment whenever the boat swayed.

Meanwhile, below the small deck canopy, Abinaya had opened the Athiyan history book she had brought from the temple library. She flipped carefully through its fragile pages, looking for anything that might relate to the island location: notes, symbols, clues.

Harish stood on the upper deck watching the horizon. But his thoughts were somewhere else. Back in the temple. Back to the moment when the priest gave Abinaya the gemstone. Why her? Why now? And why had the priest sounded so certain that the gemstone would guide them?

Harish leaned against the railing. Something told him the priest knew more about the gemstone than he had revealed. But perhaps that truth would come later.

For now, the sea demanded their attention. Hours passed as Joseph carefully navigated the dangerous waters. Reefs rose like hidden mountains beneath the surface. Several times the sonar alarm beeped sharply, warning them of sudden depth changes. Each time Joseph adjusted the course with calm precision.

Sharan finally exhaled in relief. “We’re through the worst of it.”

Joseph nodded. “Good.”

The ocean ahead looked deeper, clearer, less dangerous. Abinaya climbed onto the deck again with the book in her hands. “Any news?” Keerthi asked.

Abinaya shook her head slightly. “Nothing clear yet.” She glanced at the map coordinates Joseph had marked earlier. “We’re getting closer though.”

Joseph checked the navigation screen again. Then he looked toward the horizon. “We’re about ten nautical miles away.”

The words sent a small wave of excitement through the group. Ten miles. After centuries of silence, the lost story of Athiyan might be waiting just beneath those waters. Harish looked out across the endless blue. Somewhere ahead was a sunken island, a lost book called Kadalkol, and perhaps the first real doorway into the mystery of Kumarigandam.

The boat continued forward, cutting across the deep ocean. The wind grew stronger. The water darkened slightly. And far below the surface, something ancient waited.

But as they moved closer to the location, none of them noticed the strange sonar echo forming beneath the sea floor. Something large. Something unnatural. And it had just begun to move.
