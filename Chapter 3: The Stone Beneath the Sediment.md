# Chapter 3: The Stone Beneath the Sediment

Morning sunlight spread gently across the village of Dhanushkodi. Fishing boats rocked lazily in the shallow water. The air smelled of salt, wet rope, and the faint smoke from cooking fires beginning in the village homes.

The five friends had gathered again near the ruins of the old railway platform. The stone lay on a wooden crate between them.

But this time, they were looking at it differently. Not just as a curiosity. **As a question.**

Harish turned toward Joseph. “You never told us where exactly you found it.”

Joseph rubbed his chin slowly. His eyes moved toward the sea. “It was further out than our usual fishing area,” he said.

Keerthi leaned forward. “How far?”

“Almost two hours by boat,” Joseph replied.

That made Sharan sit up immediately. “That’s deep water.”

Joseph nodded. “The nets got tangled on something heavy.”

Harish frowned. “A rock?”

“That’s what I thought,” Joseph said. He paused for a moment, remembering. “When I pulled the net up… this stone was caught inside it.” He gently tapped the object lying on the crate. “It didn’t look special then. Just covered with mud and sea dirt.”

Abinaya studied the stone again. The carvings were still visible, but most of the surface was rough and dull. Sharan suddenly stood up. “Wait.”

Everyone looked at him. “I want to check something.”

He hurried toward his small tool bag and pulled out a handheld scanner—one of the devices he often used when repairing boat engines. But today it had a different purpose.

“Hold it steady,” he said.

Harish carefully lifted the stone while Sharan passed the scanner slowly across its surface. A soft electronic hum filled the air. Numbers appeared on the small screen. Sharan frowned. Then he scanned again. And again.

Keerthi raised an eyebrow. “What?”

Sharan didn’t answer immediately. Instead, he grabbed a small metal tool from the bag.

“What are you doing?” Abinaya asked.

“Cleaning it.”

Sharan began gently scraping away the thick layer of hardened sediment covering the stone. Tiny pieces of dried sea dirt began falling away. Joseph watched carefully. “Don’t damage the carving.”

“I won’t,” Sharan said quietly.

More scraping. More dirt falling away. Then suddenly—**a faint glimmer appeared beneath the remaining sediment.**

Keerthi leaned closer. “Did that just shine?”

Sharan froze. Very slowly, he brushed away the remaining dirt with his fingers. The surface underneath revealed something unexpected. Something bright. Something green.

Sunlight struck the stone—and reflected a deep, glowing emerald color. For a moment, nobody breathed. Sharan stared at it in disbelief.

“This… this isn’t a normal stone.”

He wiped the surface clean completely. The object in Harish’s hands now gleamed beautifully in the morning light. A rich green gemstone. Clear. Brilliant. Ancient.

Joseph whispered the name softly. ***“Maragatham.”***

Abinaya’s eyes widened. The word itself carried weight. *Maragatham*. Emerald. A gemstone known in ancient South Indian traditions for its beauty and rarity. Often worn in sacred jewelry. Associated with prosperity, growth, and grace.

In history books, such stones were said to belong to kings, temples, or ancient nobility. Never fishermen. Never lost in the sea.

Keerthi shook her head slowly. “That thing must be worth a fortune today.”

Sharan looked stunned. “Millions, maybe.”

Harish turned the gemstone carefully in his hand. The carving of the message—*For you, my love*—now looked even more beautiful etched into the glowing green surface. Someone long ago had not only written a message of love; they had written it on a gemstone of incredible value.

Abinaya whispered softly. “Whoever gave this… must have loved deeply.”

---

Keerthi suddenly leaned closer again. “Wait.”

Everyone looked at her. “What?”

She pointed to the back side of the gemstone. “Turn it.”

Harish flipped the stone slowly. The backside had been completely hidden earlier beneath the thick sea sediment. But now, with the dirt gone, something new was visible.

**More writing. Small. Carefully carved.**

Words none of them had noticed before. Abinaya leaned in immediately. “These weren’t visible earlier.”

Joseph took the stone gently from Harish’s hand. The new inscription was longer. Not a sentence—more like a fragment of something larger. Ancient Tamil again, but slightly different. Older.

Joseph’s eyes narrowed. For a moment, something stirred in his memory. “I’ve seen this before,” he said slowly.

Everyone turned to him. “Where?” Harish asked.

Joseph rubbed his forehead. “I… I don’t remember clearly.” He looked at the symbols again. His expression changed. Not confusion. **Recognition.**

“These letters…” He paused. “…someone explained them to me once.”

Abinaya’s voice was quick. “Who?”

Joseph looked up suddenly. “The temple priest.”

Keerthi blinked. “The same priest from the old temple library?”

Joseph nodded slowly. “Yes. A long time ago… he told me about inscriptions like these.”

Harish’s curiosity returned instantly. “What did he say?”

Joseph shook his head. “That’s the problem. I don’t remember everything.”

The wind from the sea blew stronger across the ruins. The gemstone sparkled brightly in the sunlight, as if it had waited centuries to be seen again. Joseph stood up.

“There’s only one way to find out.”

Harish already knew the answer. “We go see the priest.”

Sharan packed his scanner. Keerthi grabbed her bag. Abinaya carefully wrapped the *Maragatham* gemstone in cloth. The five friends began walking toward the center of the village. Toward the old temple. Toward the man who might know what the ancient inscription meant.

Behind them, the sea continued moving quietly. Patient. Watching. As if it knew the truth they were about to uncover. And perhaps—it had been waiting for this moment all along.
