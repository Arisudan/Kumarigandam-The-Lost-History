# Chapter 2: The Writing on the Stone

The stone lay between them on the sand. For a long time, none of them spoke. The moon hung low above Dhanushkodi, spreading pale light across the shore. The old railway ruins stood silent nearby, their broken walls glowing faintly in the night.

Harish picked up the stone again. It was smooth in some places, rough in others. The carvings were shallow but careful, as if someone had once spent a long time shaping every curve of the letters.

“Can you read it?” Keerthi asked, leaning closer.

Harish shook his head slowly. “It doesn’t look like the Tamil we know.”

Sharan crouched beside him. “Maybe it’s just damaged.”

Abinaya was already staring at it with deep concentration. Her fingers traced the carved shapes without touching them. “It is Tamil,” she murmured. “But very old.”

Joseph watched quietly from where he stood. The sea wind brushed through his hair as he looked out toward the dark water. “Older than the fishing villages here,” he said. “Older than our grandparents’ stories.”

Keerthi raised an eyebrow. “So what do we do now?”

Abinaya looked up. “I need time.”

---

The next morning, the village woke to its usual rhythm. Boats returned from night fishing. Children ran through sandy streets. The smell of drying nets and fresh fish filled the air.

But Abinaya walked straight toward the **old temple library**. The temple stood at the heart of the village, older than most houses around it. Its stone walls carried carvings faded by centuries of wind and salt. Inside, the library was quiet. Very quiet.

Shelves of palm-leaf manuscripts and ancient books filled the dim room. Dust floated through thin beams of sunlight that slipped through narrow windows. Abinaya had spent many afternoons here since childhood. But today felt different. Today she was searching for something specific.

She opened books carefully, scanning pages filled with ancient Tamil script. Some spoke of kings. Some spoke of trade routes and sailors who had crossed the Indian Ocean long before modern maps existed. But none explained the symbols on the stone.

Hours passed. Still nothing. Abinaya leaned back and sighed. Maybe the stone wasn’t as important as they thought. Maybe it was just an old carving lost in the sea. But something about the shapes kept bothering her. They felt… **familiar.**

Late in the afternoon, the temple priest entered quietly. He had known Abinaya since she was a child. “Still reading history?” he asked with a gentle smile.

Abinaya nodded. “I’m looking for something very old.”

The priest thought for a moment. Then he pointed toward a narrow staircase at the back of the library. “Have you ever gone down there?”

Abinaya followed his finger. The staircase led underground. “I didn’t know there was a library below,” she said.

“There used to be,” the priest replied. “Long ago.” He walked closer and handed her a small lantern. “Many of the books down there were forgotten.”

Abinaya’s curiosity sparked immediately. **Forgotten books were often the most interesting ones.**

---

The underground chamber smelled of age and damp stone. The lantern light flickered against the walls as Abinaya stepped down carefully. The room was small but filled with wooden shelves. Most of the books were wrapped in cloth to protect them from dust.

She began opening them one by one. History. Poetry. Records of temples and old villages. Then she noticed one book that looked different. Its cover was thicker. The cloth around it had faded almost completely.

Abinaya opened it carefully. The first page was written in ancient Tamil, older than most of the texts she had seen before. Her heart began to beat faster as she read the title:

> **"History of the Southern Land"**

Below the title, another name appeared: ***Kumarigandam.***

Abinaya froze. The name echoed in her mind like a distant bell. *Kumarigandam*. The lost land Joseph had once mentioned on the beach years ago. The land said to have disappeared beneath the ocean.

She turned the pages slowly. The book described a vast land south of the Indian peninsula—a place where people lived close to the sea, building cities that thrived on knowledge, trade, and ocean travel. It spoke of scholars, sailors, and poets. A civilization deeply connected to the ocean.

Abinaya read faster now. But suddenly—**the story stopped.**

Several pages had been torn out. She flipped forward. More missing pages. Entire sections gone. Only fragments of the story remained. Abinaya frowned. Why would someone remove those pages? What had they contained?

She turned to the final section of the book. And there—her breath caught. A small sketch was drawn in the corner of the page. A carved stone.

The shapes looked strangely familiar. Abinaya immediately pulled out her notebook and drew the symbols from the stone Harish had found. Then she compared them to the sketch.

**They matched.**

Her pulse quickened. She began translating the words slowly. The language was ancient but recognizable. The symbols formed a simple sentence. A quiet message. Not a warning. Not a map. Something far more human.

Abinaya whispered the translation aloud: ***“For you… my love.”***

She blinked in surprise. The stone was not a royal symbol. Not a sacred message. It was something deeply personal. A gift. Something one person had once given to another. Long ago. Very long ago.

---

When Abinaya returned to the beach that evening, the others were already waiting. Harish stood up immediately. “Did you find anything?”

Abinaya held up her notebook. “Yes.”

Everyone gathered around her. “The carving on the stone,” she said slowly. “It’s a message.”

Keerthi leaned forward. “What kind of message?”

Abinaya looked at the stone in Harish’s hand for a moment. Then she translated the words softly. “It says… *For you, my love*.”

As the words left her lips, a gentle smile appeared on her face. For just a brief moment, she looked at Harish. Not like she looked at the others. There was warmth in her eyes. A quiet affection she had never spoken aloud—the kind of feeling that had grown slowly over the years through shared sunsets and childhood memories on the shore.

Harish noticed the smile but did not fully understand it. He simply looked puzzled at the translation. For a moment, no one spoke.

Joseph chuckled quietly. “So after all that mystery…”

“Someone was being romantic,” Keerthi finished.

But Harish wasn’t laughing. He stared at the stone again. “Why would a love gift be lying at the bottom of the sea?” he asked.

Abinaya hesitated. Then she told them about the book. About *Kumarigandam*. About the missing pages. About the fragments of a lost civilization.

When she finished, the sun was already sinking into the horizon. Orange light spread across the ocean. Joseph looked out toward the waves once more. His voice was thoughtful. “If that land really existed… then the sea has been hiding its story for a very long time.”

Harish felt the familiar pull of curiosity rising again. The stone in his hand suddenly felt heavier. Not because of its weight, but because of its history. Somewhere beneath the endless water beyond Dhanushkodi, a story had been lost.

And now—a single stone had begun to bring it back.

But the ocean never gives its secrets easily. And the missing pages of history were only the beginning.
