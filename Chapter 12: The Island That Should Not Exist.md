# Chapter 12: The Island That Should Not Exist

The boat rocked gently on the dark ocean waves. The storm clouds were finally clearing, leaving behind a cold wind and the faint glow of the evening sky. All five of them sat quietly for a moment, catching their breath after the terrifying escape from the underwater temple.

Sharan handed towels to Harish, Abinaya, and Joseph. “You three look like you just came back from another world,” he said with a tired smile.

Joseph laughed softly. “It felt like one.”

Abinaya carefully placed her waterproof bag on the table inside the small cabin. Inside it rested the two things they had risked everything for: the ancient book **Kadalkol** and the rolled parchment scroll.

Harish leaned against the cabin wall, still breathing heavily. Then suddenly he looked at Abinaya. “The priest…”

Abinaya nodded slowly. They both understood the same thing at the same moment. “The gemstone,” Harish said.

Abinaya gently touched the pouch tied to her belt. “That’s why he gave it to us.”

Joseph looked at them with curiosity. “You mean he knew?”

Abinaya nodded. “He must have known that the gemstone was the key to opening the temple box.”

Harish looked out toward the dark ocean. “The priest knew more than he told us.”

Joseph folded his arms thoughtfully. “Then we need to go back to him.”

Sharan agreed immediately. “Yes. The priest might understand what’s written in the book.”

Keerthi nodded. “And maybe the scroll too.”

Joseph stood up and walked to the navigation controls. “Then we sail back to the village.”

The boat slowly turned toward the direction of Dhanushkodi. For a while, the sea was calm. But far on the horizon, dark clouds were gathering again. Joseph noticed them first. “That storm is coming straight toward us.”

Sharan checked the radar. “It’s moving fast.”

Within minutes the ocean began to roar again. Powerful winds slammed into the boat. Rain fell heavily. The waves grew taller and wilder. Joseph tightened his grip on the steering wheel. “Everyone hold on!”

Lightning flashed across the sky. The boat struggled against the violent storm. For nearly an hour they fought the waves. The compass spun wildly. The navigation signals disappeared. The boat was pushed again and again by the storm winds until finally, the rain stopped.

The waves slowly calmed. The dark clouds moved away. A strange silence returned to the sea. Joseph checked the navigation screen. Then his expression changed. “That’s… not possible.”

Sharan leaned over his shoulder. “What happened?”

Joseph pointed at the screen. “We’re not near Dhanushkodi anymore.”

Harish stepped closer. “Then where are we?”

Sharan quickly checked the coordinates. His eyes widened. “We’ve been pushed far away.”

“How far?” Keerthi asked.

Sharan calculated the distance carefully. “About 108 nautical miles from the temple location.”

Harish frowned. “That’s nearly two hundred kilometers.”

Abinaya quickly opened the modern world map on the tablet. Joseph compared it with the navigation data. Then Abinaya slowly unfolded the old Kumarigandam map they had been studying earlier. Both maps were placed side by side. 

The modern map showed only deep ocean. Nothing else. But the ancient map told a completely different story. Harish leaned closer to the old map. On it, large lands were drawn across the southern ocean—green territories filled with forests, rivers, and cities.

Abinaya traced one particular region with her finger. “This location…” She paused. “It falls exactly under the territory that once belonged to King Athiyan.”

Joseph looked closer. “What does it say?”

Abinaya read the ancient markings carefully. “This region was once the central headquarters of Athiyan’s dynasty.”

Harish’s eyes widened slightly. “And it was part of the great Chola empire's southern dominion.”

In the ancient map, the land was shown as rich and fertile, filled with greenery, cities, temples, and ports. But in the modern world, that land should not exist anymore. 

Abinaya looked back at the digital map again. “After the great ocean disasters… especially the 2004 Indian Ocean tsunami, this entire region was believed to have sunk completely beneath the sea.”

Joseph crossed his arms slowly. “So according to modern records…”

Harish finished the sentence quietly. “…this land should be underwater.”

At that exact moment, Keerthi suddenly stood up and grabbed the telescope from the deck. “Let me check something.” She climbed onto the observation platform. 

The sun was beginning to set, casting long golden light across the water. Keerthi slowly scanned the horizon. For several seconds she saw nothing but endless ocean. Then she froze. “Wait.”

She adjusted the telescope again. Far in the distance, something dark appeared above the waterline. Her voice grew louder. “I see something!”

Everyone looked up. “What is it?” Sharan asked.

Keerthi focused again. Her eyes widened. “It’s… an island.”

Joseph stood up instantly. “How far?”

Keerthi estimated the distance carefully. “Maybe four to eight miles from here.”

Harish and Abinaya looked at each other in shock. They checked the modern map again. Still nothing. No island. No land. No record. Yet right in front of them, a piece of land stood rising above the ocean—the same location that once appeared on the ancient Kumarigandam map.

Joseph slowly lowered the telescope. His voice dropped to a whisper. “That land was supposed to be lost under the sea.”

Abinaya looked toward the dark silhouette of the island. “But now…”

Harish finished the thought quietly. “…it’s right in front of us.”

Joseph turned the steering wheel slowly. “Well.” He smiled faintly. “Looks like the ocean has decided to show us something.”

The boat began moving toward the mysterious island. Night was approaching, and somewhere beyond that dark shoreline, a forgotten piece of history was waiting to be uncovered.
