### Chapter 4
The Hidden Library
The old temple stood quiet under the late afternoon sky.
Its tall stone pillars had watched over Dhanushkodi for generations. Wind carried the scent of incense and sea salt through the temple courtyard as the five friends approached.
Harish walked slightly ahead of the others.
In Abinaya’s bag, wrapped carefully in cloth, the Maragatham gemstone rested silently.
Joseph pushed open the heavy wooden door.
Inside, the temple felt cooler. The faint echo of temple bells drifted through the air.
The priest was already there.
He stood near a row of oil lamps, adjusting the wick of one of them. The warm light illuminated the calm lines of his face.
He looked up as the group entered.
“Joseph,” he said gently. “It has been a while.”
Joseph nodded respectfully.
“We need your help, Swami.”
The priest’s eyes moved from one face to another.
Then he noticed the seriousness in their expressions.
“What have you found?”
Abinaya stepped forward and slowly unwrapped the cloth.
The moment the green gemstone caught the light, the priest froze.
For a second, the room felt completely silent.
His eyes widened.
“Maragatham…” he whispered.
He lifted the gemstone carefully, turning it in his hands.
His fingers trembled slightly.
“This is no ordinary emerald.”
Harish leaned closer.
“You recognize it?”
The priest nodded slowly.
“Yes.”
His voice carried a quiet awe.
“This kind of gemstone carving belonged to an ancient royal tradition.”
Everyone exchanged glances.
Sharan spoke softly.
“Royal…?”
The priest studied the inscription again.
Then suddenly something struck him.
A memory.
A story passed down through generations.
His eyes widened further.
“This inscription…”
He paused.
“…it speaks of a lost royal couple.”
Keerthi blinked.
“A couple?”
The priest looked at them all carefully.
“This is not a simple love gift.”
He placed the gemstone gently back into Abinaya’s hands.
“This belongs to a story much older than this village.”
Joseph stepped forward.
“Is it connected to Kumarigandam?”
The priest’s expression changed.
He didn’t answer immediately.
Instead, he turned and walked toward the back of the temple library.
“Come with me.”
The group followed him down the familiar staircase that Abinaya had explored the day before.
The underground chamber appeared again—the same room filled with old wooden shelves and ancient books wrapped in cloth.
Abinaya looked around.
“This is where I found the book yesterday.”
The priest nodded.
“Yes.”
But he didn’t stop there.
Instead, he walked toward the far wall of the room.
He moved aside an old wooden shelf.
Behind it—
A narrow stone passage appeared.
Everyone stared in surprise.
Keerthi whispered, “There’s another room?”
The priest lit a lantern.
“This path has been protected by temple priests for generations,” he said quietly.
“It is not meant for everyone.”
Joseph looked around carefully.
“What is inside?”
The priest gave a small smile.
“The deeper memory of our past.”
They stepped into the hidden passage.
The air felt cooler.
The tunnel was narrow, its stone walls smooth with age.
The lantern light flickered across the path as they walked.
Then the passage opened.
And the friends stopped in their tracks.
Before them stretched a vast underground chamber.
Shelves upon shelves of carefully preserved books filled the room.
Palm-leaf manuscripts.
Ancient scrolls.
Heavy bound volumes.
All arranged with incredible care.
It looked like another world.
An entire forgotten library.
Abinaya’s eyes shone with wonder.
“This… this is incredible.”
The priest nodded proudly.
“These records have been protected by our temple for centuries.”
He gestured toward the endless shelves.
“They contain the histories of dynasties, scholars, and kingdoms that once existed in the southern lands.”
Harish looked around slowly.
“And Kumarigandam?”
The priest turned toward a large wooden cabinet.
“Yes.”
He opened it and carefully lifted out a thick, beautifully preserved book.
Its cover was dark and heavy with age, yet perfectly maintained.
The priest placed it gently on a stone table.
“This book tells the story of one of the greatest kings of the southern lands.”
He opened the first page.
Written across it in elegant ancient Tamil letters was a name.
Athiyan.
Sharan leaned closer.
“Who was he?”
The priest began turning the pages.
“King Athiyan ruled over the Seven Eastern Coastal Territories.”
He pointed to a passage.
“In ancient records, this region was known as Ezhu Kuna Karai Nadu.”
Abinaya’s voice filled with excitement.
“That’s one of the seven groups of territories!”
The priest nodded.
“Yes.”
He traced a map drawn on the page.
“Kumarigandam was once divided into forty-nine territories, governed by the Pandiyan kings.”
He paused.
“These territories were grouped into seven regions. Ezhu Kuna Karai Nadu was one of them—an important coastal kingdom.”
Joseph listened carefully.
“So Athiyan ruled one of these regions?”
“Yes,” the priest replied.
“And he was remembered not only for his power…”
He turned another page.
“…but for his love.”
The page contained a beautiful illustration of a royal couple.
“Athiyan loved his queen deeply,” the priest continued.
“Her name was Mathi.”
Keerthi smiled softly.
“A love story.”
The priest nodded.
“Mathi loved him just as deeply.”
He read from the book quietly.
“Their story was known across the territories—a symbol of loyalty and devotion.”
As the priest spoke, Abinaya found her eyes drifting toward Harish.
For a moment, she imagined what such a love must have felt like.
Strong.
Unspoken.
Enduring.
Harish, unaware of her thoughts, was focused on the book.
Nearby, Sharan noticed Keerthi watching the illustration with interest.
He smiled slightly.
Keerthi caught his glance and raised an eyebrow.
The two quickly looked away, pretending nothing had happened.
Some feelings lived quietly between friendships.
Waiting for the right moment to be understood.
The priest turned another page.
Then suddenly—
Something slipped out from the middle of the book.
A piece of aged parchment.
Joseph quickly picked it up.
It wasn’t a page.
It was part of a map.
The parchment showed a section of land surrounded by ocean.
Strange markings covered the surface.
And one corner of the map had been torn away long ago.
Sharan stared at it.
“A map fragment?”
The priest looked troubled.
“This map was believed to be lost.”
Harish felt his heartbeat quicken again.
“Lost… from where?”
The priest studied the fragment carefully.
Then he looked up at the group.
“This map was said to lead to something hidden by King Athiyan himself.”
Silence filled the underground chamber.
The lantern flame flickered.
The gemstone in Abinaya’s bag seemed to glow faintly beneath the cloth.
The priest closed the book slowly.
“If this fragment has appeared again…”
He paused.
“…then the story of Athiyan and Mathi is not finished.”
Harish looked at the map fragment.
A strange excitement rose in his chest.
Somewhere out there in the ocean—
A lost kingdom was waiting.
And perhaps…
The first clue had just been found.
But the map was only a fragment.
And the ocean still held the rest of the story.
